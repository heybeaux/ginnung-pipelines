# 5 minutes of emotion

I love that my blog is mostly fun and exciting stories. I love that my mother cringes when she reads some of the things I write. I love that other travel writers read my blog and encourage me because they believe I'm a gifted storyteller. However, this post is a little more serious. This post is about my tears, and not my laughter.

When I left China, I figured that that was it. I had had my fun, made my friends, and whatever happened there after I left wouldn't have the slightest impact on me. As it turns out, travellers don't leave places behind as easily as tourists. It seems that as a traveller, I left a very big piece of my heart with my newfound family in the Shaolin Village. I had no idea until yesterday.

On this lazy Sunday afternoon I was preparing to go to Central Festival in Phuket. Central is a massive stupid-market and a great way to waste hours of your day whilst feeling as if you're still at home in a western country. Coffee, ice-cream, clothing stores. It is such a generic layout that as an Aussie, I felt right at home, as if I were still in Brisbane. Before I left, I jumped onto my Facebook feed and checked my friend's status updates.

Someone liked their sandwich…

Someone liked that someone liked their sandwich…

Someone wanted a sandwich…

Someone couldn't spell sandwich…

Reading such intelligent updates made me think "Dear God, I'm pretty sure that social media is making us dumber". It was then that I stumbled across an update from my beautiful friend, my Mama Bear, my Iya. Iya and I grew close while I was in China. She would challenge my views on life and would call me on my bullshit. She trusted me with her son as if I was his father. She loved me… No… Loves me, so openly and honestly. We are from very different walks of life, but we connected in such a raw and honest way. I'm sure we always will be.

Iya posted a heartfelt update on a close friend of mine becoming a disciple of our Shifu. As she explained in her post, this isn't something to be taken lightly. It is an honour. The bond between Shifu and disciple is akin to that of a father and son. A Shifu doesn't just teach his disciple Kung Fu. He teaches him his own way of life. It is a lifelong commitment. What's more, is that this sort of honour is rarely bestowed upon a foreigner. To make the event even more significant, my friend would be my Shifu's first ever disciple. There were so many reasons for this to be a momentous occasion. Because of her relationship with my French friend, Mama was asked by our "Little Monkey" to stand in for him in the traditional Chinese ceremony. Those who know Mama and Little Monkey know that those two would go to the ends of the earth for the other.

As I read Mama's status update, I felt tears welling in my eyes. I had no idea that after I left China, I would be drawn back so strongly. I had no idea that such a special moment in a friend's life would make me feel so… Proud. I was proud that Little Monkey had achieved so much. I was proud at all he had achieved. I was proud that he had formed such a positive and meaningful relationship with Mama. I was upset that I wasn't in China to see this. I was upset that I wasn't there to share in such a special moment. As the weight of these different thoughts and feelings hit me… I began to cry. I cried openly, so full of joy that my Shaolin family were growing and developing is such a beautiful way and achieving so much.

In such a short time I had come to recognise these people as family. I was so proud that I couldn't contain the tears.

Congratulations Little Monkey. You deserve this honour. You are such an amazing guy with a big heart. I wish I was there to give you a big hug. It's the least I could do to show you how proud I am of you.
