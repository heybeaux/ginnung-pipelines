# The night bus

Vientiane.

The bus station smelled like diesel and fish sauce and something sweet I never identified. There was a queue, sort of. More like a loose agreement that people were standing in a general direction. I joined the back of it and that's where I met Sophie.

Sophie was French, about 5'5", carrying a backpack that looked like it weighed more than she did. She said "Allo" and I said "G'day" and we both sort of laughed because neither of us had much else to offer. Her English was functional. My French was non-existent. We established the basics. Name. Country. Where are you going. How long have you been travelling. The conversational checklist every backpacker runs through in under 90 seconds.

Then they opened the bus.

Now. I need you to understand what "VIP sleeper" means in Laos. It does not mean what those words mean. Separately or together. Someone had taken a regular bus and bolted bunk beds into it, two across, stacked floor to ceiling, running the full length of the vehicle. Each bunk was maybe 5'10" long. I am 6'2". And each bunk was designed (I use that word generously) for two people.

Two strangers.

Lying down.

Together.

Sophie looked at the bunk. She looked at me. She looked back at the bunk. She said something in French that I didn't understand but the tone was very clear. I climbed in first because my legs were the problem and I figured if I angled myself diagonally I could almost fit. Sophie climbed in after me and we lay there side by side, our shoulders touching, staring up at a ceiling that was about 40 centimetres from our faces whilst the engine shook the whole frame like it was trying to rattle the bolts loose.

The bus pulled out of the station at 7pm. We had 10 hours ahead of us.

Here is something no one tells you about travel. Not the blogs, not the guidebooks. No one mentions the specific, excruciating intimacy of sharing a bed with someone you met 20 minutes ago whilst hurtling through mountains in the dark. You are close enough to hear them breathe. You can feel their body heat. Every time one of you shifts, both of you shift. There is no personal space. The concept doesn't exist. Personal space is a luxury for people who aren't on a converted bus in northern Laos.

The road from Vientiane to Luang Prabang is switchbacks. All of it. Every single kilometre. And the driver drove like the road had wronged him, like every corner was a personal insult he needed to answer with violence. He would accelerate into the curves and then brake at the last possible moment, throwing everyone sideways. Sophie would slide into me. I would slide into the wall. The whole bunk would creak. Fifteen minutes later, same thing, other direction.

About two hours in, Sophie's elbow ended up in my ribs after a particularly aggressive turn. She said "Pardon" and I said "All good" and then we both just started laughing. Quietly at first. Then properly. The kind of laughter that comes from the total absurdity of your situation when you can't even see each other's faces. We couldn't communicate beyond basic phrases. We were in a country where neither of us could read the signs out the window. And we were sharing a bed on a bus in the mountains at night.

What else are you going to do but laugh?

There were maybe 30 other people on that bus, all in their little bunks, and the whole vehicle had this strange ecosystem going on where some were snoring, some were watching movies on phones with the brightness turned up to a level that should be illegal, and somewhere near the back two blokes were having a conversation in what I think was German. The whole bus smelled like Tiger Balm and feet. Every now and then the driver would honk his horn at absolutely nothing and I'd jolt awake and Sophie's hand would be on my arm and I couldn't tell if that was accidental or reassuring and it didn't matter.

The bus stopped at 3am.

I don't know where we were. A mountain. Somewhere in the middle of Laos. The driver turned the lights on and said something over a speaker system that was so distorted it wouldn't have mattered if I spoke fluent Lao. But the message was clear enough. Everyone was getting out.

It was raining. Not heavy rain. That fine, warm rain that Southeast Asia does at altitude, the kind that gets into everything without you noticing. I stepped off the bus and the air hit me and I have never felt more awake. My feet were on gravel. There were mountains on either side of us, just these massive dark shapes against a slightly less dark sky. No lights. No buildings. Just the bus, the rain, and 30 odd travellers fanning out to find somewhere to pee.

There is no dignity in this moment. None. You just pick a direction, walk far enough that you feel you've respected some arbitrary boundary, and go. Sophie went left. I went right. I stood on the side of a mountain in Laos at 3am, pissing into the dark in the rain, and I remember thinking this is the most alive I've felt in years.

Not skydiving. Not the temples. Not the sunsets everyone photographs. Standing on gravel in the rain with a busload of strangers.

I got back on and Sophie was already in the bunk. She'd taken the wall side this time, which meant my legs could hang off the edge slightly. A small kindness. A negotiation conducted entirely without language. She said something quiet in French as I climbed in. I have no idea what it was. I said "Yeah" because that felt like the right response to anything at that point.

We lay there in the dark whilst the bus pulled back onto the road and the driver resumed his war with the corners. Sophie fell asleep first. I could tell because her breathing changed and she stopped bracing against the turns. Her shoulder was against mine. The rain was on the roof.

I never saw Sophie again after Luang Prabang. We got off the bus at 5am, both looking like we'd been dragged through something. She said "Merci" and kissed me on both cheeks in that French way and I said "No worries" and meant it completely. We didn't exchange numbers or emails or Facebook names. We just existed together for 10 hours in a space too small for one person and then went our separate ways.

I think about that bus more than I think about any temple I visited. More than any waterfall or market or plate of food. I think about laughing in the dark with someone whose language I didn't speak. I think about the mountain at 3am.

Sounds awful.

It was perfect.
