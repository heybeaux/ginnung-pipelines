# Power stretching

Two weeks in.

Two weeks of getting up at 5:30, running the mountain trail in the dark, doing forms until my legs shook, and eating whatever mysterious protein turned up at the canteen each night. I was feeling good. Properly good. The kind of good where you catch your reflection in the bathroom mirror and think "yeah, alright, we're doing this". I had blisters on my feet and calluses forming on my knuckles. I could get through a full basics session without wanting to die. I was, in my own humble and completely deluded opinion, getting fit.

Then Brother Tom found me.

I call him Brother Tom because his Chinese name had about four tones I couldn't reproduce without sounding like I was choking on something. Tom was one of the senior students. Quiet guy. Maybe 22, maybe 35… honestly impossible to tell. He had the posture of a ballet dancer and the emotional range of a brick wall. He approached me after morning training, smiled politely, and said through the translator something along the lines of "You come now. Stretching."

Stretching. I could do stretching. I'd been touching my toes for years. Well. My shins. Close enough.

Tom led me across the courtyard to a smaller training room I hadn't been in before. The mats in there were older, darker, compressed from years of use. The smell hit you immediately… that particular cocktail of sweat, tiger balm, and whatever industrial cleaner they used that somehow made things smell worse than if they'd used nothing at all. Two other students were already waiting. They looked at me the way a mechanic looks at a car that's just rolled in making a weird noise. Diagnostic. Patient.

Nobody spoke.

Tom gestured for me to sit on the floor and I did, legs out in front of me, thinking this was going to be a nice cool-down situation. A reward, even. A gentle wind-down after two hard weeks. One of the students knelt behind me and placed his hands on my shoulders. The other sat facing me and took hold of my ankles. And then… they just started.

There was no countdown. No "ready?" No "this might be a bit uncomfortable". One pushed. One pulled. My chest went towards the floor and my legs went in directions that I'm fairly certain legs aren't supposed to go without prior written consent.

I made a noise. I'd love to tell you it was a manly noise. A grunt. A warrior's exhale. It was not. It was the noise a dog makes when you accidentally step on its paw. High pitched. Surprised. Deeply undignified.

Tom stood to the side, arms folded, watching. Not concerned. Not amused. Just... observing.

They held me in that position for what felt like an hour but was probably thirty seconds, then let me come up for air briefly before pushing me back down again, further this time. I could feel muscles in my hips and lower back that I genuinely did not know existed. Muscles that had apparently been living rent-free in my body for 25-odd years and were now being evicted without notice.

The translator (God bless her) leaned down next to my ear at some point during the second or third round and said, very gently, very politely, "You need to relax your breathing."

I was crying.

Not figuratively. Not "my eyes watered a bit". Actual tears running down my face into the old mat that smelled like feet and tiger balm. My body had made the decision to cry without consulting me. It wasn't even pain exactly (it was also pain). It was something else. Shock, maybe. The total loss of control. Someone else was in charge of where my body went and how far, and all I could do was breathe and try not to make that dog noise again.

I failed. I made the dog noise several more times.

Here's the thing that messed with me afterwards. Tom and the two students weren't being cruel. They weren't even being particularly intense about it. This was Tuesday for them. They did this every day, to themselves and to each other, and probably had since they were children. What was, for me, the most physically confronting experience of my life was for them just... the bit before lunch.

They moved me into a side split next. One student on each leg, slowly walking my feet apart whilst I sat there with my palms flat on the mat, trying to breathe, trying to convince my groin muscles that this was fine and we were all friends here. Brother Tom knelt in front of me and pressed down on my thighs. He caught my eye and for the first time I saw something that might have been warmth. Or pity. Possibly amusement. He said one word that I didn't need translated.

"Breathe."

So I breathed. And cried. Simultaneously. A real multitasker.

The whole session lasted maybe 40 minutes. When they finally let me stand up I felt like a different person, and not in a motivational poster way. In a literal, physical way. Like someone had taken me apart and put me back together with the pieces in slightly different spots. I walked out of that room bow-legged, wiping my face with my shirt, and two of the younger students in the courtyard looked at me and just nodded. They knew. They'd been through it.

I went back to my room and sat on my bed for a while. I thought about how two weeks earlier I'd looked in that bathroom mirror and thought I was fit. Fit. What a joke. I had a children's birthday party understanding of what my body could do. A bouncy castle version. Balloons and fairy bread. Meanwhile these blokes had been stretching their bodies into origami since age six and didn't even consider it remarkable.

The thing I keep coming back to, years later, isn't the pain. The pain faded. It's the fact that I never would have done that to myself. Never. Not in a million years. If someone had handed me a sheet of paper that said "today you will do a full side split whilst crying in a room that smells like feet" I would have politely declined and gone back to touching my shins and feeling proud about it. I needed someone else to make that decision for me. I needed Tom and those two quiet students to calmly, wordlessly push me past the point where my brain said "no, this is where we stop, this is bullshit, we're going home".

And nothing bad happened. My legs didn't snap off. I didn't die. I just cried a bit and walked funny for a day.

I went back the next morning. And the morning after that. By the end of my time at the academy I could do things with my body that the version of me who arrived (the bloke who thought touching his shins counted as flexibility) would have called physically impossible.

All it took was a quiet bloke named Tom and a room that smelled like feet.
