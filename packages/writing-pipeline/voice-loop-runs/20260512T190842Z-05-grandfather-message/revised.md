Tuesday. Coles car park. Engine off, windows up, phone in my hand.

My grandfather sent me a voice message. He's 84 and he has never sent one before. I don't mean he hasn't sent one in a while. I mean the man has owned a mobile phone for maybe six years and I'm fairly certain he still calls it "the bloody thing".

The message was three minutes long. Most of it, a solid two and a half minutes, was him trying to work out if it was recording. You could hear him breathing, tapping the screen, saying "hello?" like the phone might answer back. At one point he said "oh for Christ's sake" under his breath and I could hear what I think was him putting it down on a table and picking it back up again.

Then right at the end, in this slightly impatient voice like he was annoyed at himself for even doing this, he said: "I just wanted to tell you I'm proud of you, and I hope you're well, and I won't be around forever so I thought I should say it."

Then he hung up. That was it.

I listened to it three times. Sat there in the car with the air con clicking and trolleys rattling past and someone's kid screaming about a Freddo Frog. Three times. Not because I missed something. Because I didn't know what to do with it.

The men in my family don't do this. That's not a complaint, it's just the architecture of the house I grew up in. My grandfather once drove eleven hours to help my dad fix a water tank and they barely spoke the whole weekend. That was love. Showing up was the language. You mowed someone's lawn. You lent them your ute. You picked their kids up from school without being asked. You didn't say the word "proud" out loud because that would be too much, too bare, like walking outside without your skin on.

Pop (I've always called him Pop, everyone does) grew up on a cattle property outside Emerald. Left school at fourteen. Married Nan at twenty. Worked for fifty years and never once in my entire life told me he was proud of me to my face. He'd tell Nan. Nan would tell Mum. Mum would tell me. That was the system and it worked fine. The words arrived, they just took the scenic route.

So what happened on Tuesday? I keep thinking about that. What made an 84 year old man pick up a phone he barely understands and fumble through three minutes of frustration just to say one sentence? Was it a doctor's appointment? A mate's funeral? Did he just wake up and think today's the day? I don't know. I'll probably never ask him, because asking would mean acknowledging that the thing happened, and acknowledging that the thing happened would mean we'd have to talk about it properly, and talking about it properly isn't something either of us has the stomach for.

Here's the thing that got me though. It wasn't the words. The words were good, obviously the words were everything. But it was the fumbling. Those two and a half minutes of him fighting with the technology, because that was the hard part for him. Not saying the thing. Getting to the point where he could say it. He fought through a piece of shit device that makes no sense to him, that he doesn't trust, that he would happily throw at a wall on most days, just to reach me. The obstacle was the message before the message.

I sat in that car park for maybe twenty minutes after the third listen. Thought about calling him back. Didn't. Thought about sending a voice message of my own but couldn't bring myself to do that either because I knew I'd sound like an idiot trying not to cry in a Coles car park and that's not really the energy I wanted to transmit. Texted Mum instead and said "Pop sent me something nice" and she wrote back "Oh that's lovely" with a full stop, which for Mum is practically weeping.

I eventually sent him a text. Kept it short because he would have wanted it short. Said thanks Pop, love you, talk soon.

He wrote back two hours later. "Ok."

That's my family. That's how we do it. A man breaks the unspoken rule of forty years on a Tuesday morning and we all quietly absorb it and keep moving. Nobody makes a fuss. The fuss would ruin it.

But I saved that voice message and backed it up twice. I reckon I'll listen to it again on days when I need to hear an old man clear his throat and swear at his phone and then, almost as an afterthought, say the biggest thing he's ever said to me.

Three minutes. Two and a half of them useless.

The other thirty seconds I'll keep forever.
