# The quiet bit

Coffee.

That's where it starts. Every single time. The kettle, the cup, the little ritual of pouring. You'd think the writing starts when your fingers hit the keys but it doesn't. Not even close. The writing starts somewhere around the first sip, when you sit down, open the laptop, watch the cursor blink, and then don't write.

You scroll. You check something. You read back over the last thing you wrote and convince yourself it's either brilliant or garbage. You get up. You wash a dish. Not all the dishes, just one, just enough to feel like you did something productive so the guilt doesn't eat you alive. Then you come back, sit down, look at the screen again.

Nothing.

So you make a second coffee.

I used to think this was procrastination. I used to sit there in that dead space between intention and action and feel genuinely guilty about it, like I was wasting time, like every other writer on the planet just sat down and went and I was the one broken unit who couldn't get the engine to turn over. My partner has walked past my desk more than once and seen me staring at a blank screen whilst petting the dog and thought yeah, that bloke's not working.

But here's the thing. I was.

I just didn't know it yet.

There's this moment that nobody really talks about. Not the typing, which is the loud bit, the productive bit, the bit you screenshot for Instagram. I mean the moment before. The moment where you've committed. You've decided you're writing today. The laptop's open. The document's there. The coffee is going cold. And you're just sitting in it. Waiting. For what? Something has to land first. Some shape of a sentence, some angle into the idea that wasn't there five minutes ago, and you can feel it circling like a dog that won't lie down but you can't grab it yet.

So you walk the dog. The actual dog.

And somewhere between the front door and the end of the street, it lands. A first line. An image. A rhythm. Something clicks into place and suddenly you know how to get in. Not what you're going to say (that comes later, messily, in real time) but how to start saying it. The door into the room.

Then you go. And it pours out. And you look back at the clock and realise you wrote 800 words in 25 minutes and it took you an hour and a half to get there, which is either deeply inefficient or the most efficient thing in the world depending on how you look at it.

I think we've been lied to about what productivity looks like. Or maybe not lied to, maybe we just absorbed it through osmosis, this idea that work has to be visible, that if you're not typing you're not writing, that the gap between sitting down and producing words is a failure of discipline. There are whole books about this. Whole YouTube channels. Some bloke with perfect posture telling you to just start, just write anything, even if it's bad, because momentum is everything.

And sure. Sometimes that works. Sometimes you can brute force your way through the quiet bit and come out the other side with something decent. But the stuff I force out never reads like the stuff I waited for. The forced stuff is technically fine. It has structure and it makes sense and if you squint it looks like proper writing. But it doesn't have that thing. That energy. That feeling of the words having arrived rather than been assembled, like they existed already and I just happened to be the idiot who finally sat still long enough to hear them.

I know this sounds wanky. I'm not sitting around waiting for my muse to descend from the heavens wrapped in silk. I'm washing a dish. There's a difference.

What I've figured out, and it took me an embarrassingly long time to figure out, is that the quiet bit is the work. The sitting. The scrolling. The second coffee. The dog walk. All of it. That's when the brain is doing the shit it actually needs to do before the fingers can move. It's sorting through angles that won't work and discarding them one by one, circling closer and closer to the thing I actually want to say until finally it finds the thread.

And I pull.

I've stopped feeling guilty about it. Properly stopped. Not in that way where you tell yourself you've stopped but still feel a twinge every time you get up from the desk to put the kettle on. I mean actually stopped, because I started paying attention and noticed something. The days I fought through the quiet bit, I'd write 500 words and delete 400 of them the next morning. The days I let the quiet bit breathe, I'd write 800 and keep 750.

The maths is pretty bloody clear.

So if you sit down to write and the words don't come straight away, that's fine. That's the first part. The invisible part. You're not procrastinating. You're loading. And when the sentence lands, you'll know, because it'll feel less like thinking and more like remembering, and then you go.

But first, the coffee.
